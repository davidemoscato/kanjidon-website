The 5 Best Apps to Learn Kanji in 2026

You've decided to learn kanji. Great choice—and terrible timing for your sanity. With over 2,000 characters to memorize just to read a newspaper, you'll need a solid digital ally. But which app should you choose among dozens available? Here's an honest comparison, no sugarcoating.

What to look for in a kanji app

Before diving into reviews, let's clarify what makes an app truly effective:

  • Spaced Repetition (SRS) — The only scientifically proven method for long-term memorization
  • Variety of exercises — Reading, meaning, listening, writing—the more angles you attack a kanji from, the better you'll remember it
  • JLPT coverage — From N5 to N1, without switching apps mid-journey
  • Built-in motivation — Streaks, goals, rewards—something that keeps you coming back daily
  • Accessible pricing — Because learning Japanese is already demanding enough

1. Duolingo

The verdict: Great for starting out, insufficient for kanji.

Pros

  • Attractive, gamified interface
  • Perfect for absolute beginners
  • Free (with ads)
  • Covers grammar, vocabulary, and conversation

Cons

  • Kanji are treated as an afterthought, not a focus
  • No dedicated SRS system for individual characters
  • Can't study specific kanji—you must follow the linear path
  • Limited coverage (doesn't even reach JLPT N3)

Best for: Those who want a taste of Japanese before committing seriously. For kanji, you'll need to supplement with something else.

2. Anki

The verdict: Incredibly powerful, but requires a master's degree in setup.

Pros

  • One of the best SRS algorithms out there
  • Completely customizable
  • Thousands of community-shared decks
  • Free on desktop and Android

Cons

  • Dated interface that looks like 2005 (because it is)
  • Steep learning curve—before studying kanji, you need to study Anki
  • iOS app costs $25
  • Community decks vary wildly in quality
  • Zero gamification—studying can get boring

Best for: Advanced learners who know exactly what they want and have patience to spare.

3. WaniKani

The verdict: Excellent method, premium price, English only.

Pros

  • Radical system that breaks kanji into memorable pieces
  • Creative (sometimes hilarious) mnemonics
  • Structured path from zero to 2,000+ kanji
  • Active and supportive community

Cons

  • Costs ~$9/month or $299 for lifetime access
  • English only—if you're not fluent, the mnemonics won't work
  • Rigid study pace—you can't speed up even if you already know some kanji
  • No official app (web only or third-party apps)
  • Miss a few days and reviews pile up overwhelmingly

Best for: Native English speakers with budget who love mnemonic storytelling.

4. Kanji Study

The verdict: Great dictionary, less effective as an active study tool.

Pros

  • Complete and detailed kanji database
  • Stroke order animations
  • Works offline
  • One-time purchase (no subscriptions)

Cons

  • More a dictionary than an active learning app
  • Basic quizzes without variety
  • No gamification or motivational elements
  • Android only

Best for: Those looking for quick reference or want to look up specific kanji. As a primary study tool, it's limited.

5. Kanjidon

The verdict: The modern approach—serious gamification meets scientific SRS.

Pros

  • 8 different quiz types—meaning, on/kun reading, listening, writing, and more
  • SM-2 algorithm—the same scientifically validated SRS system used by millions of students
  • Collect kanji like cards—each character becomes a tangible item to unlock
  • Real-time PvP battles—challenge other learners and climb ranks from E to S
  • 21 languages supported—study in your native language
  • Complete coverage—from kana to JLPT N1
  • Free with full basic features, optional premium
  • Native iOS and Android apps, works offline

Cons

  • Doesn't use narrative mnemonics (if you love them, WaniKani might suit you better)
  • PvP battles require internet connection
  • Relatively new app compared to established competitors

Best for: Those who want a modern, engaging, and complete experience. Perfect if you learn better through active practice and gamification rather than memorizing stories.
