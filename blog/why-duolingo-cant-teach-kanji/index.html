Can Duolingo Teach You Kanji? Strengths and Limits

By Deborah, Kanjidon Team · Updated Jul 17, 2026 · 10 min

Yes, Duolingo teaches kanji. Its Japanese course introduces characters through words and includes a dedicated kanji tab with recognition, tracing, writing, and component practice. The more useful question is whether that approach is enough for your goal. For casual Japanese it may be; for systematic JLPT coverage or character-level review, you may want a second tool.

What Duolingo Does Well

Before criticizing, let's recognize the merits. Duolingo does some things better than anyone else:

  • Accessibility: anyone can start in 30 seconds, for free, without knowing anything
  • Gamification: streaks, hearts, leaderboards. It keeps you coming back every day, and that's worth its weight in gold
  • Basic vocabulary: Learn useful words in context, with native audio
  • Introductory Grammar: The basic structures of Japanese are introduced in an intuitive way
  • Hiragana and katakana: the course teaches them well in the early levels

Japanese asks learners to build vocabulary, grammar, and three writing systems at the same time. Duolingo's broad course design makes that first step approachable and introduces kanji gradually instead of presenting hundreds of isolated characters at once.

Where Duolingo's Approach Differs

Duolingo teaches kanji mainly as part of words and sentences. That is useful for context: a character's reading and meaning become connected to vocabulary you have already met. It is different from a kanji-first curriculum organized around radicals, isolated readings, or JLPT lists.

The kanji tab adds focused recognition, writing, and component exercises, so it would be inaccurate to say Duolingo does not teach characters. The trade-off is control: the course decides when kanji appear, while dedicated tools usually let you choose a JLPT level, inspect readings and mnemonics in depth, and review each character on its own schedule.

What You Can and Cannot Expect

Progress after six months varies too much to promise a universal number. Your starting level, lesson pace, use of the kanji tab, and outside reading all matter. A more honest comparison is by outcome:

  • Duolingo is useful for meeting kanji inside beginner vocabulary and sentences
  • The character tab reinforces shapes, readings in known words, writing, and visual components
  • The course is not organized as a complete JLPT kanji checklist
  • Learners have less control over isolated character reviews than in a dedicated SRS
  • Knowing a kanji in one word does not automatically teach every common reading or usage

If your target is general beginner Japanese, Duolingo can be a useful core habit. If your target is a specific JLPT level or durable recall of each character, add a tool that exposes coverage and review status explicitly. No responsible app can guarantee an N4 result from a fixed number of minutes alone.

Four Limits to Understand

1. Components Are Support, Not a Full Radical Curriculum

Duolingo now includes component exercises that help learners notice how similar characters are built. That is useful visual practice, but it is not the same as a complete radical-led curriculum with reusable names and mnemonics for every component. Learners who rely heavily on decomposition may want a specialist resource.

2. Less Control Over Character-Level Review

Duolingo schedules practice across the course and provides dedicated character exercises, but it does not expose the same granular queue controls as a kanji-specific SRS. If you repeatedly confuse 書 and 読, a specialist tool can make those exact items visible and reviewable without waiting for the broader lesson path.

3. Readings Are Learned Through Words

Duolingo deliberately connects a kanji to the words you have encountered. This avoids memorizing readings with no vocabulary context, but it also means one familiar word may not prepare you for another reading of the same character. A reference-rich kanji app makes those alternate readings and examples easier to inspect together.

4. Mnemonics Are Not the Main Method

Some learners remember characters better with explicit visual stories that connect components, meaning, and sound. Duolingo emphasizes repeated contextual exercises instead. Neither technique is universally best, but if a character repeatedly fails to stick, a mnemonic-focused tool can provide a different memory cue.

You Do Not Need to Give Up Duolingo

Duolingo introduces grammar, vocabulary, listening, sentences, and writing systems in one polished routine. The limitation is not that the app is useless for kanji; it is expecting one broad course to provide the depth of every specialist resource.

Think of it as one tool in a study kit. A broad course, a focused review system, and real reading practice solve different problems.

A Practical Two-Tool Routine

One practical approach is to combine tools by job instead of duplicating the same kind of exercise:

  • For kanji: a specialist app with character-level SRS, JLPT structure, and mnemonics. Kanjidon covers the 2,136 joyo kanji with 16 quiz types; kana and N5 content are free.
  • For grammar: Duolingo (basic) + a book like Genki or Tae Kim (in-depth)
  • For vocabulary: Duolingo + graded reading (NHK Easy News, graded readers)
  • For listening: anime with Japanese subtitles, podcasts, YouTube videos
  • For conversation: tutor on Italki (when you're ready, not right away)

A simple 30-minute example is 15 minutes of focused kanji review and 15 minutes of broader grammar or vocabulary practice. Adjust the split to your goal and review load; the point is to give each session a clear purpose, not to promise a result from a timer.

What Really Works for Kanji

If reading is a priority, look for these qualities in a dedicated kanji tool:

  • SRS at the single kanji level: the algorithm tracks each character individually
  • Radicals as a base: You learn the components before complex kanji
  • Mnemonics: Visual stories for every kanji, in your language
  • Explicit ON/KUN readings: You always know which reading you are learning
  • JLPT Organization: From N5 to N1, you always know where you are
  • Variety of quizzes: flashcards alone are not enough, different exercises are needed

The honest conclusion is that Duolingo can teach and reinforce kanji, especially for beginners, but it serves a broader goal than a specialist JLPT or SRS tool.
