Kanji vs Chinese Characters: Differences Explained

"If I learn Japanese kanji, can I read Chinese?" It's a common question, and the answer is... complicated. Kanji derive from Chinese characters, but after centuries of separate evolution, there are significant differences.

The common origin

Kanji (æ¼¢å­—, literally "Han characters") were imported from China to Japan between the 4th and 6th centuries AD. At that time, they were practically identical to classical Chinese characters.

Since then, however, three things have changed:

  1. Japan developed its own variants
  2. China simplified many characters (1950s-60s)
  3. Meanings evolved differently

Can I read Chinese if I know kanji?

The honest answer:

  • Traditional Chinese (Taiwan): You can understand ~50-60% of the general meaning of written text, but can't pronounce it
  • Simplified Chinese (China): You can understand ~30-40%, many characters are different
  • Spoken: Zero. Pronunciations are completely different
