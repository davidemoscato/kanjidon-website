Kanji vs Hanzi: Differences, Readings and False Friends

By David, Kanjidon Team · Updated Jul 17, 2026 · 13 min

Japanese kanji and Chinese hanzi share a writing-system ancestry, but they do not make Japanese and Chinese mutually readable. A reader may recognize a character, guess a topic, or notice a related word while still missing the pronunciation, grammar, and intended meaning. This guide separates the genuine overlap from the shortcuts that cause mistakes.

Shared Origins, Different Languages

Writing in Chinese characters reached Japan in antiquity and was adapted to represent Japanese. Kanji acquired Sino-Japanese readings called on'yomi and native Japanese readings called kun'yomi, while kana developed for grammatical endings and other sounds. Modern Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, and other languages can therefore use related Han characters without sharing pronunciation or sentence structure.

Five Differences That Matter

1. The Form of Characters

Japan's postwar shinjitai and mainland China's simplified characters are separate standards. Some modern forms coincide, some remain traditional in Japanese, and some differ in all three columns. Taiwan and Hong Kong generally use traditional forms, with regional conventions of their own.

The useful lesson is not that Japanese sits neatly between two Chinese systems. It is that each character must be checked: 国 and 学 coincide in Japanese and simplified Chinese, 風 remains traditional in Japanese, and 竜 is a distinctly Japanese standard form beside 龍 and 龙.

2. The Readings: The Biggest Gap

A shared written form does not imply a shared sound. Japanese kanji commonly have multiple on'yomi and kun'yomi, and the reading is learned through words. Chinese characters are not limited to one pronunciation either: some are polyphonic, and pronunciations differ across varieties such as Mandarin and Cantonese.

For example, Japanese 生 appears in words such as 生活 (seikatsu, life), 生まれる (umareru, to be born), and 生 (nama, raw). Mandarin 生 is often read shēng, but it also participates in language-specific compounds and usages. The safest unit to learn is the whole word, not a character-plus-one-sound rule.

3. Related Characters Can Form False Friends

Even when the characters are recognizable, a compound may have developed a different everyday meaning. The Chinese column below uses common modern Mandarin wording, including simplified forms where relevant.

ExpressionJapaneseModern Mandarin
手紙 / 手纸Letter, correspondenceToilet paper
勉強 / 勉强StudyReluctantly; to force or compel
大丈夫Okay; all right; safeA great or principled man
汽車 / 汽车Train, especially a steam trainCar; automobile
新聞 / 新闻NewspaperNews

These are vocabulary differences, not proof that one language uses the characters incorrectly. Treat familiar-looking compounds as hypotheses and confirm them in a Japanese or Chinese dictionary before relying on the apparent meaning.

4. Japan-Created Characters (Kokuji)

Japan also created characters known as kokuji. They can be unfamiliar to Chinese readers, although a few were later borrowed or documented outside Japan, so 'never exists in Chinese' is too absolute. Their Japanese readings and uses still need to be learned as Japanese vocabulary.

  • 峠 (tōge) — mountain pass
  • 辻 (tsuji) — crossroads
  • 畑 (hatake) — cultivated field
  • 榊 (sakaki) — the sacred sakaki tree
  • 働 (hataraku) — to work; a Japanese-created character later used beyond Japan

5. Grammar Changes Everything

Recognizing every kanji still does not decode the sentence. Japanese uses kana for particles such as は, が, を, and に, as well as verb and adjective endings. In 食べられなかった (could not eat), 食 supplies the lexical core while the following kana carry important morphology. Word order and grammatical marking also differ from Chinese.

Can a Chinese Reader Understand Japanese?

Sometimes a Chinese-literate reader can infer the topic of a sign, menu label, or headline from shared characters. That is not the same as reading the Japanese sentence. Kana, inflection, Japanese word meanings, and readings make exact comprehension unreliable without Japanese study.

MaterialWhat may transferWhat still blocks reliable reading
Short sign or labelA familiar character may reveal the topicJapanese-specific words and intended nuance
HeadlineSeveral content words may be recognizableCompounds, names, omitted context, and pronunciation
News articleSome nouns and broad themesGrammar, kana, inflection, and language-specific vocabulary
Conversation or novelOccasional isolated charactersFull sentence structure, voice, style, and idiom

Existing hanzi knowledge can reduce the visual novelty of some kanji and provide useful semantic clues. The size of that advantage varies by the reader's language, literacy, and exposure. Japanese readings, grammar, and vocabulary remain separate learning tasks.

Can a Japanese Reader Understand Chinese?

The same limitation works in the other direction. A Japanese reader may identify familiar characters or guess a subject, but Chinese syntax, vocabulary, pronunciation, and character standards prevent dependable reading. It is also unsafe to claim that one direction is universally easier: the result depends on the text, script variety, and reader.

Which Should You Learn First?

Choose the language you actually want to use. If Japanese is the goal, learn kanji through Japanese words, readings, kana, and sentences. If Mandarin is the goal, learn hanzi with Mandarin pronunciation, words, and grammar. Shared forms can become helpful cross-language anchors later, but merging the two systems too early encourages false readings and false friends.

A Practical Comparison Checklist

  • Check whether the form is traditional Chinese, simplified Chinese, Japanese shinjitai, or shared
  • Learn pronunciation from a word in the target language, not from the character alone
  • Confirm familiar-looking compounds in a language-specific dictionary
  • Read the surrounding kana or Chinese grammar before inferring the sentence
  • Record false friends and visually similar variants as explicit contrasts
