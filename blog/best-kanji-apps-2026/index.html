7 Best Kanji Apps in 2026: Free, SRS & JLPT Picks

By David, Kanjidon Team · Updated Jul 17, 2026 · 12 min

The best kanji app depends on what you need: a guided path, flexible flashcards, handwriting practice, JLPT structure, or motivation to return every day. This comparison matches seven popular options to those jobs instead of pretending one score fits every learner. Disclosure: Kanjidon is our app, so its entry is clearly presented from the product team's point of view, not as an independent award.

How We Compared These Apps

We reviewed current product information on July 17, 2026 and compared the same practical criteria for every option:

  • Learning method: SRS, active recall, mnemonics, writing, or contextual practice
  • Curriculum: kanji coverage, JLPT organization, and freedom to skip known material
  • Daily experience: setup time, feedback, motivation, and ease of review
  • Access: supported platforms, offline use, and language availability
  • Value: what is available free and how the paid model works

Kanjidon: Best for a Gamified JLPT Path

Kanjidon combines character-level spaced repetition, collectible cards, JLPT organization, and competitive features. Because we build Kanjidon, consider this the product team's explanation of who it is designed for, not an independent verdict.

What We Loved

  • 16 quiz types test recognition, readings, writing, listening, and context
  • Character-level spaced repetition schedules targeted reviews
  • Beautiful collectible card design makes each kanji memorable
  • Real-time PvP battles add motivation and competition
  • JLPT levels (N5-N1) perfectly organized from day one
  • Multilingual interface and mnemonic support
  • Free access to kana and JLPT N5 content
  • No advertising
  • Native iOS and Android apps with offline mode

What Could Be Better

  • No web version (mobile only)
  • Some advanced features require Premium

Bottom line: Kanjidon is a strong fit if you want a mobile-first JLPT path with more variety and game-like motivation than traditional flashcards. You can evaluate the complete kana and N5 learning loop for free before deciding whether Premium is useful.

WaniKani: Best for a Fixed Mnemonic Curriculum

WaniKani has earned its reputation as a serious kanji learning platform. Its radical-mnemonic system works well for learners who prefer highly structured, guided experiences.

What We Loved

  • Comprehensive radical + kanji + vocabulary system
  • Creative English mnemonics for each character
  • Strong community and third-party tool ecosystem
  • Proven track record with thousands of successful learners

What Could Be Better

  • Paid plans cost $9 monthly, $89 annually, or $299 lifetime as of July 2026
  • No official mobile app (web-based only)
  • Rigid pace, can't skip what you already know
  • English-only interface
  • Free tier limited to first 3 levels only

Bottom line: WaniKani is excellent if you have the budget, prefer web-based learning, and want a strictly guided path. Not ideal for learners who want flexibility or mobile-first experience.

Anki: Best for DIY Power Users

Anki is a flexible flashcard system rather than a ready-made kanji curriculum. It can support an excellent study plan, but the learner is responsible for choosing or building the content.

What We Loved

  • Completely free on desktop and Android
  • Infinitely customizable
  • Works for any subject, not just kanji
  • Massive library of community-created decks
  • Mature spaced-repetition scheduling

What Could Be Better

  • More setup and deck maintenance than a dedicated kanji app
  • The official iOS app is paid
  • Deck quality varies wildly
  • No gamification or motivation features
  • Motivation and curriculum depend on the deck you choose

Bottom line: Anki is powerful for learners who want maximum control and are willing to verify deck quality. Beginners can still use it successfully, but a well-designed deck matters more than the app alone.

Kanji Study: Best Android Reference and Writing Tool

Kanji Study combines a deep kanji and word reference with flashcards, configurable quizzes, writing challenges, custom sets, and an optional Guided Study SRS module. It is one of the most complete Android-focused options in this comparison.

What We Loved

  • Excellent kanji lookup and search
  • Detailed information for each character
  • Good stroke order animations
  • Free beginner content with optional one-time upgrades and add-ons

What Could Be Better

  • Full guided SRS is a separate add-on
  • Less game-like than Kanjidon or Duolingo
  • Android only (no iOS version)
  • The depth of options can feel dense to a new learner

Bottom line: A particularly strong choice for Android learners who care about handwriting, lookup depth, and customization. Whether it is a supplement or your main tool depends on whether you add Guided Study.

Duolingo: Best for a Broad Japanese Introduction

Duolingo teaches Japanese as a whole and now includes a dedicated kanji tab with recognition, tracing, writing, and component exercises. Its strength is making a broad beginner routine easy to start, not providing a kanji-only JLPT curriculum.

What We Loved

  • Dedicated practice for hiragana, katakana, and kanji
  • Kanji are introduced through words in the main course
  • Gentle introduction to Japanese overall
  • Highly polished, addictive experience
  • Generous free tier

What Could Be Better

  • Kanji study follows the broader course rather than a kanji-first path
  • Less control over character-level review than dedicated SRS tools
  • No JLPT level organization
  • Subscription pricing varies by plan and market

Bottom line: Good for building a broad Japanese habit. If your priority is systematic JLPT kanji coverage, detailed readings, or granular SRS, use it alongside a dedicated kanji tool rather than assuming one app must do everything.

Remembering the Kanji: Best for a Meaning-First Method

RTK is a classic method that teaches kanji meanings through imaginative stories. The app companion helps, but the method has significant limitations.

What We Loved

  • Systematic approach to all 2,136 joyo kanji
  • Creative mnemonic stories
  • Strong community of RTK followers

What Could Be Better

  • Teaches meanings only, no readings
  • Requires separate study for pronunciation
  • Book + app together cost $30+
  • Method is controversial (meanings without readings)
  • Dated app interface

Bottom line: Only recommended if you're committed to the RTK method specifically. Most learners are better served by apps that teach meanings and readings together.

Kanji Tree: Best for Simple Casual Practice

Kanji Tree is a simple, no-frills kanji app. It does the basics competently but lacks the features that make dedicated study effective.

What We Loved

  • Simple, clean interface
  • Free tier available
  • JLPT level organization

What Could Be Better

  • Basic SRS implementation
  • Limited quiz variety
  • No gamification or motivation features
  • Small development team means slow updates

Bottom line: Adequate for casual study, but serious learners will quickly outgrow it.

How to Choose

Choose for your bottleneck. Pick Kanjidon if you want a gamified, mobile JLPT path; WaniKani if you want a rigid mnemonic curriculum; Anki if you want total control; Kanji Study if Android handwriting and reference depth matter most; or Duolingo if you want one gentle routine for Japanese overall.

Try the free portion of two finalists for one week using the same daily target. Keep the one that makes reviews clear and repeatable. Feature lists matter, but a study system only works if you can sustain it.

Whatever you choose, consistency matters more than a universal ranking. The best app is the one whose method matches your goal and whose review queue you will actually complete.
